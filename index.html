What are Patta and Chitta?

Patta is a legal record issued by the land revenue department that proves ownership of a particular parcel of land. It contains the owner’s name, survey number, extent and other ownership-related information. Chitta (also called adangal or village account) lists the land classification, cultivation details, and area information. Together these documents form the authoritative land record for rural and semi-urban properties.

Why download Patta & Chitta?

Having downloaded copies of your land documents helps in many scenarios: property transactions, bank loans, succession claims, government schemes and legal verification. Digital downloads are also useful because they:

  • Provide a time-stamped snapshot of the record for your file
  • Help verify that records held by local offices match official databases
  • Reduce the need to visit revenue offices for simple checks

Before you start — what you need

To successfully find and download your records you should have basic details ready:

  • District, taluk (tehsil) and village name
  • Survey number or patta number (if known)
  • Owner/occupant name (useful if numeric identifiers are missing)
  • A smartphone or computer with internet access and a PDF reader

Step-by-step: How to download Patta & Chitta

  1. Visit the official portal or a trusted guide: Start with your state’s revenue department website or a centralized resource such as patta chitta land records which provides links and step-by-step instructions for each state.
  2. Select your state and district: Choose the correct administrative region. Many portals list every district and taluk clearly.
  3. Find the search form: Look for options such as “Patta Search”, “Adangal / Chitta”, “View Land Records” or “RS-1 / 14”.
  4. Enter required details: Input the survey number, patta number, or owner name. If you only have partial information, try different combinations (owner name + village).
  5. View results: Once the record appears, verify the owner name, survey number and area match your expectation.
  6. Download / Print: Most portals offer a “Download” or “Print to PDF” button. Use that to save an official-looking copy. If a direct download button is not available, use your browser’s Print → Save as PDF option.
  7. Check document authenticity: Confirm government seals, serial numbers or digital signatures when present. Keep screenshots of the search page as additional proof of how the record appeared at the time.
Note: Different states use different terms and formats — for example, Tamil Nadu uses “Patta” and “FMB” / “Adangal”, while other states may call similar records RS-1B, 14/12, or 7/12 extracts.

Troubleshooting common issues

If you can’t find your record online:

  • Double-check spelling of village and owner name and try variations.
  • Use survey number or old patta number if available — numbers are the most reliable search keys.
  • Portals sometimes undergo maintenance. Try again later or use cached guides from trusted resources.
  • If records seem missing or incorrect, visit your local revenue office to request a correction or certified copy.

Security and authenticity

Always download records from official government domains (typically ending in .gov.in) or use reputable guide portals that link to the official service. For transactions, banks and legal parties will usually require certified or office-stamped copies — online downloads are a good first step but may not replace certified originals in every case.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the downloaded patta legally valid?
Downloaded copies are useful for reference. For legal transactions most institutions prefer certified copies from the revenue office or digitally signed documents from the government portal.
Can I download Patta & Chitta for any property?
You can download records that are available in the public database. Some recent updates or pending mutations may not appear immediately online.
What if the online record is incorrect?
Contact your local revenue office with supporting documents to request a correction or mutation. Keep copies of online searches and any receipts you receive.
